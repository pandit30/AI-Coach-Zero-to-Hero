## Question 1 — Type: Concept Check

Why does the session say the question "is current AI AGI?" cannot be answered with a simple yes or no? What are at least three different competing definitions of AGI?

**What to look for:** The problem: nobody agrees on what counts as "human-level intelligence," so the answer depends entirely on which definition you use. Three definitions from the session: (1) Economic: AI that can do 50% of all economic tasks humans currently perform (OpenAI's working definition); (2) Cognitive: AI that performs at or above PhD level on any cognitive task; (3) Test-based: AI that passes a Turing Test robustly in any domain; (4) Learning: AI that can learn any new skill as fast as a human from the same experience (François Chollet's definition — described as "the hardest definition"); (5) Autonomy: AI that can autonomously set goals, plan, and execute across any domain. The session's example: o3 passes Definition 2 on some benchmarks but completely fails Definition 4. Is it AGI? Depends on which definition you use.

## Question 2 — Type: Concept Check

The Google DeepMind "Levels of AGI" framework replaces the binary AGI question with a five-level scale. Describe at least three levels and where current AI (as of 2025) sits.

**What to look for:** From the session: Level 0 = No AI (calculators); Level 1 = Emerging (equals unskilled human performance — GPT-3, early chatbots); Level 2 = Competent (equals 50th percentile of skilled adults — "we are HERE for most tasks" with GPT-4o, Claude 3 Sonnet); Level 3 = Expert (equals 90th percentile of professionals — o3 on specific domains like math and coding); Level 4 = Virtuoso (99th percentile — not clearly achieved for any domain yet); Level 5 = Superhuman (surpasses all humans — chess/Go engines yes, general cognitive no). Current AI: at Level 3 (expert) in narrow domains like code generation and scientific reasoning benchmarks, but Level 1–2 for many others.

## Question 3 — Type: Scenario

Your board asks: "Should we be worried that AGI will make ECHO India's product irrelevant in 5 years?" How do you answer using the range of expert predictions and the "honest bottom line" from the session?

**What to look for:** Should convey: expert predictions span 3 years (Altman, Hassabis: 2027–2030) to never (LeCun, Gary Marcus). The session's "honest bottom line": "Nobody knows. The disagreement spans 3 years to never. Plan around uncertainty, not a specific date." For ECHO India specifically: the session's practical position is "plan for 2–3x capability improvements in the next 3–5 years as a near-certainty; treat transformational AGI as a possibility worth preparing for but not worth betting on." HR decisions require accountability, auditability, and human judgment — design ECHO India's products to augment these human strengths. The human HR professional of 2030 will focus on judgment, culture, and ethics — things that resist automation.

## Question 4 — Type: Application

What are the four concrete product strategy implications the session draws from the AGI debate — regardless of when or whether AGI arrives?

**What to look for:** From the session's "Why This Matters for Product Strategy Now" section: (1) Plan for rapid capability change — design products so you can swap in a more capable model when one arrives; don't bake in workarounds for current limitations; (2) The automation question is "when and which tasks," not "if" — each capability jump makes more tasks automatable; build at the layer above automation (strategy, judgment, relationships); (3) Safety and reliability now — as AI becomes more capable and autonomous, cost of failures scales; build human oversight, audit trails, and explainability now while systems are still limited; (4) The talent equation — skills in AI product development have a long runway of value whether AGI is 5 or 30 years away.

## Question 5 — Type: Concept Check

The session distinguishes between things current top models (o3, Gemini 2.5 Pro, Claude 3.7) can do at human-expert level, and things they still cannot do. What are the two most important limitations that keep them from being considered "general" AGI?

**What to look for:** From the session's honest assessment: Things they CANNOT reliably do: (1) Learn new skills from 1–3 examples — "humans can; current AI cannot without fine-tuning." This is François Chollet's ARC-AGI challenge — the ability to learn any new skill from minimal data, which is perhaps the hardest AGI definition; (2) Operate autonomously over weeks on complex, multi-step real-world tasks — current agents fail on extended autonomous operation; (3) Reason about physical causality reliably; (4) Demonstrate genuine common sense in novel physical scenarios; (5) Self-improve without human intervention. Students should get at least two. The honest conclusion: we are at AGI by narrow benchmark standards, not by general capability standards.

## Question 6 — Type: Scenario

A colleague claims "ASI is just sci-fi and not worth thinking about." How would you characterise the serious arguments for AND against taking ASI seriously — without either dismissing it or overhyping it?

**What to look for:** Arguments for taking it seriously (from session): (1) Intelligence is, to a significant degree, the ability to acquire more intelligence; (2) Computing resources are not the bottleneck; (3) The trajectory from GPT-2 to o3 in 5 years is remarkable. Arguments for skepticism: (1) We have very little understanding of what intelligence fundamentally is; (2) Each generation required massive human engineering effort — it doesn't self-improve automatically; (3) Hard limits may exist (compute costs, training data availability, alignment); (4) Physical world constraints don't disappear just because software improves. The session's practical position: "Treat AI as a rapidly improving tool. Plan for capability levels 2–3x current arriving in 2–4 years. Don't plan around sci-fi scenarios — but do take capability improvement as a given and build adaptably."

## Question 7 — Type: Application

The session says ECHO India should "start collecting outcome data now" as a response to the AGI debate. Why does this advice follow from understanding how AI capabilities improve — even if AGI never arrives?

**What to look for:** The connection: the session explains that as AI capabilities grow, the company with better outcome data can train better models. Whether AGI arrives in 5 years or 50, AI improvement requires reward signals — which require outcome measurement. For ECHO India: data on which hires worked out, which attrition was avoidable, which policies reduced engagement — these are the reward signals for building progressively better HR AI. This creates a compounding competitive moat: more data → better AI → better decisions → more clients → more data. The insight: you cannot catch up on historical outcome data; the time to start collecting it is now, before you need it for training. This is the AlphaGo lesson applied to HR (from Session 115) but expressed in the AGI strategic context.
