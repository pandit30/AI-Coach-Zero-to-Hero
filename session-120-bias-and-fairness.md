## The Key Idea

AI bias is not a bug that gets introduced by careless engineers — it is the predictable output of training on data generated by an unequal world. When you train a model on historical hiring decisions, historical loan approvals, or historical medical diagnoses, you are teaching the model to replicate the patterns in that history — including the discrimination embedded in it. Understanding where bias enters the pipeline, and what "fair" actually means mathematically, is one of the most important things a PM building AI products needs to know.

## Type 1 — Historical Bias

The world is unequal. Historical data reflects that inequality. A model trained on this data learns to replicate — and amplify — the existing inequality.

**Example:** An Indian bank trains a credit scoring model on 10 years of loan repayment data. Historically, women in rural areas were approved for fewer loans (due to existing bias in loan officers) and had smaller loan sizes. The model learns that women in rural areas are "worse credit risks" — not because they actually are, but because the historical data shows lower credit limits and fewer loans to that group. The model then continues denying them credit, reinforcing the original pattern.

This is called a **feedback loop** — the model's decisions become the future data it is trained on.

---

## Type 2 — Representation Bias

When certain groups are underrepresented in training data, models perform poorly on those groups.

**Facial recognition: the canonical case.** The MIT Media Lab's 2018 "Gender Shades" study by Joy Buolamwini found that commercial facial recognition systems from IBM, Microsoft, and Face++ had:
- Error rates under 1% for light-skinned males
- Error rates up to 35% for dark-skinned females

The cause: training datasets were overwhelmingly composed of light-skinned male faces. The models worked well for the majority and failed systematically for minorities.

This isn't abstract. When police departments use facial recognition with these error rates, and the error rate is highest for Black women, that is a real-world harm.

---

## Type 3 — Measurement Bias

What gets measured, and how accurately, affects what the model learns.

**Healthcare AI example:** A 2019 study published in Science found that a commercial algorithm used by US hospitals to allocate healthcare resources was systematically undertreating Black patients. The algorithm used healthcare costs as a proxy for healthcare needs — the logic being that sicker patients cost more. But Black patients in the US have historically had lower access to healthcare and therefore lower healthcare costs, even when equally sick. The model learned "lower costs → less sick" and recommended less care for Black patients. The cost data was accurately measured; it was just the wrong thing to measure.

## The Amazon Hiring Algorithm: The Landmark Case

In 2014, Amazon built an AI tool to screen CVs for technical roles. By 2018, it was scrapped internally after discovering systematic bias against women.

**What happened:** The model was trained on 10 years of CVs from successful Amazon technical hires. Those hires were overwhelmingly male — because the tech industry had been predominantly male for decades. The model learned that male patterns were associated with success and penalised female indicators:
- Downgraded CVs that included the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club")
- Penalised graduates of all-women's colleges
- Learned to prefer action verbs that appeared more in men's CVs ("executed," "captured") over those that appeared more in women's CVs

Amazon never deployed the tool — they caught it. But the same dynamic happens in tools that are deployed and never audited.

## COMPAS: When Bias Has Criminal Consequences

COMPAS (Correctional Offender Management Profiling for Alternative Sanctions) is a recidivism prediction tool used in US courts to help judges decide on bail and sentencing.

In 2016, ProPublica published an investigation showing:
- Black defendants were **twice as likely** to be falsely flagged as high risk (future recidivism)
- White defendants were more likely to be falsely flagged as low risk when they went on to reoffend

The tool was using factors correlated with race (zip code, family criminal history, employment status) to predict recidivism — without using race directly. The output was racially biased because the proxies were racially correlated.

The company (Northpointe) responded that the tool had equal predictive accuracy across races — and they were correct, by one definition of fairness. ProPublica was also correct, by a different definition of fairness. This is the core of the fairness paradox.

## The Fairness Paradox: Definitions That Cannot All Be True Simultaneously

This is the hardest part of bias in AI. There are multiple mathematically precise definitions of fairness, and it is **proven impossible** to satisfy all of them at the same time when base rates differ across groups.

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              FOUR FAIRNESS DEFINITIONS — AND WHY THEY CONFLICT       │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                      │
│  1. DEMOGRAPHIC PARITY                                               │
│     Equal positive outcome rates across groups.                     │
│     "30% of both Group A and Group B get hired."                   │
│     Problem: ignores whether groups have different qualifications    │
│                                                                      │
│  2. EQUAL OPPORTUNITY                                                │
│     Equal true positive rate across groups.                         │
│     "Of all qualified candidates, equal % get hired regardless      │
│      of group."                                                      │
│     Problem: may still produce different overall hiring rates        │
│                                                                      │
│  3. PREDICTIVE PARITY (CALIBRATION)                                 │
│     Equal precision across groups.                                  │
│     "When the model says 'high risk,' it's right equally often      │
│      across groups." (Northpointe's defense in COMPAS)              │
│     Problem: may have different false positive rates across groups   │
│                                                                      │
│  4. INDIVIDUAL FAIRNESS                                              │
│     Similar individuals should be treated similarly.                │
│     Problem: requires defining "similar," which itself embeds       │
│     assumptions                                                      │
│                                                                      │
│  THE IMPOSSIBILITY RESULT: When base rates differ across groups,    │
│  you cannot simultaneously achieve demographic parity,              │
│  equal opportunity, AND predictive parity.                          │
│  You must choose. And that choice is a values decision, not a       │
│  technical decision.                                                 │
│                                                                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

**This is critical for PMs to understand:** You cannot delegate the fairness definition to the data science team. "Which definition of fair do we use?" is a product and ethics decision that has real consequences for real people.

## What a PM Can Do: The Practical Toolkit

**Before building:**
- Define which definition of fairness matters for this use case. Document it.
- Map which groups could be harmed by errors. For each group, what does a false positive cost? A false negative?
- Audit your training data for representation: what percentage of your data comes from each demographic group?

**During development:**
- Require disaggregated metrics: don't just report overall accuracy. Report accuracy/precision/recall broken down by gender, geography, age bracket, caste category (where legally appropriate).
- Test for proxy discrimination: run the model on synthetic data that varies only one protected attribute. Does the output change?
- Build a fairness dashboard into your model monitoring.

**After deployment:**
- Monitor for drift in fairness metrics over time — a fair model at launch can become biased as the world changes.
- Create a feedback channel for users to report perceived bias.
- Set a review cadence (quarterly at minimum for high-stakes decisions).

## What This Means for ECHO India

ECHO India operates in HR tech — one of the highest-risk domains for AI bias. Indian-specific dimensions add complexity.

**Caste as a proxy:** A hiring model trained on historical data from Indian companies may learn that candidates from certain colleges (IITs, top-tier private universities) are more successful. But college selectivity in India is correlated with caste, class, and geography. "Tier 1 college" is a proxy for caste — and a model that penalises non-IIT graduates may be indirectly discriminating by caste.

**Gender in performance management:** Historical performance ratings in Indian companies have gender patterns — not because women perform worse, but because they were evaluated through biased criteria. Training on historical ratings amplifies those patterns.

**Geography bias:** Rural vs urban, North vs South, state-specific dialects in natural language models — all can create representation bias in any AI system ECHO India builds.

**Regulatory risk:** The Digital Personal Data Protection Act 2023 and forthcoming AI governance rules in India are moving toward requiring fairness documentation for automated decisions affecting employment. Starting the fairness audit practice now is ahead of the compliance curve.

**Concrete action for ECHO India:** For any AI feature that affects hiring, promotion, or performance — ECHO India should require a "fairness memo" before launch: what definition of fairness was chosen, what disaggregated metrics were tested, and who reviewed them.

## Key Takeaway

Bias in AI is not an accident — it is the predictable result of training on data from an unequal world. It enters at every stage of the pipeline: data collection, feature engineering, model training, and deployment feedback loops.

The landmark cases — Amazon hiring, COMPAS, facial recognition — all follow the same pattern: a model that was technically performing well by aggregate metrics was causing systematic harm to specific groups.

There is no neutral definition of fairness. Choosing which fairness definition to optimise for is a values decision that belongs to product leadership, not the data science team alone. As a PM, your job is to force that decision to be made explicitly, documented, and reviewed.
