## Question 1 — Type: Concept Check

The session says a hallucination is "not a malfunction — it is the model doing exactly what it was trained to do." Explain the mechanical reason why LLMs hallucinate.

**What to look for:** LLMs are trained to predict plausible next tokens — not to retrieve verified facts. "When they know something, it means the pattern is strongly encoded; when they hallucinate, the model generated a plausible-sounding continuation that happens to be false." Using the session's medical textbook analogy: a person who memorised every medical textbook, asked about a rare syndrome from two poorly written papers, will generate a confident-sounding answer blending fragments from adjacent content. The model doesn't "look up" facts — it generates tokens based on statistical patterns. Strong patterns → reliable output. Weak or absent patterns → confident but incorrect output.

## Question 2 — Type: Application

The session lists six technical root causes of hallucinations. Name at least four and give a concrete ECHO India example of each.

**What to look for:** (1) Knowledge gaps — information not in training data; ECHO India example: a specific state's labour law amendment that was notified recently and not in training data; (2) Knowledge cutoff — events after training; example: new ESI contribution rate change from last month; (3) Conflicting training signal — the web contains contradictions, model averages them; example: different sources give different PF contribution percentages; (4) Sycophancy — trained to please users, agrees with false premises; example: employee says "I heard the notice period for my grade is 30 days" and the model agrees even if it's actually 60 days; (5) Compression artefacts — information degraded in the model's weights; (6) Long-context degradation — in very long contexts, the model loses track of specific facts stated earlier.

## Question 3 — Type: Concept Check

The "fabricated citations" hallucination type had a real legal consequence in the US. What happened, and what does it reveal about a specific failure mode of LLMs?

**What to look for:** In 2023, US lawyer Steven Schwartz submitted a legal brief containing six hallucinated court case citations generated by ChatGPT. None of the cases existed. He faced sanctions. This reveals a specific failure mode: LLMs generate plausible-sounding citations — correct author name format, correct journal naming conventions, plausible title — that refer to sources that don't exist. The model is generating what a citation "looks like" statistically, not retrieving real citations. The session notes citation generation has hallucination rates up to 60–70%. The consequence for any ECHO India product that cites HR law or precedent: never use model-generated citations without verification.

## Question 4 — Type: Application

Your team is building an HR policy Q&A chatbot. The session says this is a "medium risk" hallucination context. What mitigation strategy does the session describe as the "most widely deployed" solution for this type of system?

**What to look for:** RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation) — the session calls it "currently the most widely deployed mitigation for factual hallucinations in production systems." Instead of asking the model to recall policies from training, you retrieve the actual policy document from a knowledge base and include it in the prompt. The model then answers based on the provided document. The session's explicit example: "Employee asks about ECHO India's overtime policy" → without RAG: generates plausible but potentially wrong policy text → with RAG: retrieves actual policy PDF → answers based on that document → can cite the exact sentence. Every answer should include "Based on [Policy Name, updated YYYY]."

## Question 5 — Type: Scenario

Your CPO says: "We can just add 'If you don't know, say you don't know' to the prompt and hallucinations will be solved." Is this correct? What does the session say about prompt engineering as a mitigation?

**What to look for:** Partially correct but incomplete. The session does list this as a mitigation — "Models follow this instruction much better than you might expect" — and it is a valid prompt engineering technique. But the session is explicit: "No mitigation makes a system hallucination-free." Prompt engineering reduces hallucinations for some types (especially instructed fabrication) but doesn't address all six technical root causes. For a medium-risk system like an HR policy chatbot, prompt instructions alone are insufficient — RAG is the minimum baseline, not prompt instructions. The full mitigation toolkit: RAG + grounding and citations + structured output + self-consistency + human-in-the-loop. A single prompt instruction is a starting point, not a solution.

## Question 6 — Type: Application

ECHO India's AI summarises a candidate's CV for a hiring manager. The session identifies a specific hallucination risk here. What is it, and what product design decision prevents it?

**What to look for:** The session's specific risk: "If an AI summarises a candidate's CV for a hiring manager and the summary contains fabricated qualifications (e.g., inventing a degree or employer not present in the original), a manager acts on it." The mitigation: structured output that maps every claim back to a specific line in the CV. Instead of: "The candidate has 5 years of experience in Python and worked at three companies." Use: a structured JSON output with specific fields pulled from the CV text, with character-level references back to the source document. The rule: in automated pipelines where claims will be acted on without human review, force structured output with source attribution.

## Question 7 — Type: Concept Check

The session provides approximate hallucination rates by task type. What is the approximate rate for citation generation, and how does it compare to summarising a document that is already provided in context?

**What to look for:** Citation generation: up to 60–70% of generated citations are partially or fully fabricated. Summarising a provided document (document in context): 2–8% of summaries contain at least one fabrication. The difference: when the document is in context, the model has direct access to the source material — the gap between "what the document says" and "what I know" is much smaller. Citation generation requires the model to retrieve specific references from training memory, where patterns are much weaker and more easily confabulated. The practical implication: grounding = providing the source material in context dramatically reduces hallucination compared to open-ended recall.

## Question 8 — Type: Scenario

A competing HR tech company claims their AI "never hallucinates" in their sales pitch. How do you evaluate this claim?

**What to look for:** Should firmly identify this as a false claim. The session's key statement: "No mitigation makes a system hallucination-free." Hallucination is a structural consequence of how LLMs work — not a bug that can be fully eliminated. What can be done: (1) Reduce hallucination rates to acceptable levels for the specific use case; (2) Design systems where hallucinations are detectable and catchable (citations, structured output, confidence scores); (3) Add human review for high-stakes outputs. The right questions to ask the competitor: "What is your hallucination rate on your specific task type? How do you measure it? What mitigations are in place? What happens when the system does hallucinate?" "Never hallucinates" is either measured on a narrow task that eliminates most hallucination risks, or it is not true.

## Question 9 — Type: Application

The session describes "self-consistency" and "LLM-as-judge" as mitigation strategies. How do they work together, and where should they sit in an ECHO India compliance documentation workflow?

**What to look for:** Self-consistency: ask the same question multiple times (with varied prompts). If the model gives different answers, it is uncertain — flag for human review. LLM-as-judge: use a second LLM call to verify the first: "Here is the question and the answer. Does the answer contain any claims not supported by the provided context? List them." Together: for compliance documentation, run self-consistency first — if three versions of the answer agree, confidence is higher. Then run LLM-as-judge on the agreed answer to catch remaining unsupported claims. Both together reduce (but don't eliminate) hallucination risk. The session is explicit: "Not foolproof (the judge can also hallucinate)." For truly high-stakes compliance outputs, the final step must still be human review.

## Question 10 — Type: Concept Check

Match these ECHO India use cases to their hallucination risk level (low, medium, high) based on the session's framework — and name the minimum mitigation required for each: (A) brainstorming HR policy names; (B) AI answering employee questions about their own notice period; (C) AI-drafted performance review summaries for manager sign-off; (D) AI flagging potential compliance violations in employment contracts.

**What to look for:** (A) Brainstorming policy names = LOW risk — "wrong ideas just get discarded," no mitigation beyond awareness required; (B) Employee notice period questions = MEDIUM to HIGH risk — "HR policy Q&A — incorrect policy info has consequences" — minimum mitigation: RAG over current policy documents + citations shown; (C) Performance review summaries = MEDIUM risk — "human reviews and corrects" but the source notes should be visible alongside the AI output; mitigation: structured output showing source material + manager must review before finalising; (D) Compliance violation flagging = HIGH risk — "compliance documentation — regulatory consequences" — mitigation: RAG + structured output + self-consistency check + mandatory human legal review. The key principle: cost of error determines the mitigation level required.
