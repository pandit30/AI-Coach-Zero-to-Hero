## The Key Idea

An LLM hallucination is not a malfunction — it is the model doing exactly what it was trained to do, just in a case where the training generalises incorrectly. LLMs are trained to predict plausible next tokens; they are not databases that retrieve stored facts. When they "know" something, it means the pattern is strongly encoded; when they hallucinate, it means the model generated a plausible-sounding continuation that happens to be false. Understanding this mechanically is the foundation for building AI products that handle hallucinations properly.

---

## Why LLMs Hallucinate: The Mechanical Explanation

**The analogy:** Imagine you have memorised every medical textbook written before 2024, plus millions of medical forum posts, case studies, and papers. You've read so much that you can complete almost any medical sentence fluently. Now someone asks you a question about a rare syndrome that appeared in only two papers you read, both poorly written. You don't remember the answer clearly — but you're trained to respond fluently. You will generate a confident-sounding answer that blends fragments from adjacent, related content. That answer will be wrong in specific details.

This is what an LLM does. It doesn't "look up" facts. It generates tokens based on statistical patterns from training. Strong patterns → reliable output. Weak or absent patterns → confident but incorrect output.

**The technical root causes:**

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              WHY HALLUCINATIONS HAPPEN                               │
├──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┤
│                                                                      │
│  1. KNOWLEDGE GAPS                                                   │
│     Information not in training data → model interpolates           │
│     from adjacent knowledge → wrong but plausible answer           │
│                                                                      │
│  2. KNOWLEDGE CUTOFF                                                 │
│     Events after training → model has no signal → either           │
│     refuses or generates plausible-but-false content                │
│                                                                      │
│  3. CONFLICTING TRAINING SIGNAL                                      │
│     Web contains contradictions → model averages them out →        │
│     generates a blend that matches nothing precisely                │
│                                                                      │
│  4. SYCOPHANCY                                                       │
│     Trained to please users → if user seems confident about        │
│     a false premise, model agrees → a form of hallucination        │
│                                                                      │
│  5. COMPRESSION ARTEFACTS                                            │
│     Billions of tokens compressed into billions of parameters →    │
│     some information is degraded, garbled, or merged with          │
│     adjacent information                                             │
│                                                                      │
│  6. LONG-CONTEXT DEGRADATION                                         │
│     In very long contexts, attention dilutes → model loses track   │
│     of specific facts stated earlier in the context               │
│                                                                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Types of Hallucinations

Not all hallucinations are the same. They differ in how they manifest and how dangerous they are.

**Factual hallucination:** The model states a fact that is simply wrong.
- "The Indian Companies Act requires board approval for purchases above ₹5 lakh." (Made-up threshold)
- "The paper by Gupta et al. (2019) found..." (Paper may not exist)

**Fabricated citations:** The most notorious type. LLMs generate plausible-sounding citations — correct author name format, correct journal naming conventions, plausible title — that refer to papers that do not exist.
- In 2023, a US lawyer (Steven Schwartz) submitted a legal brief containing six hallucinated court case citations generated by ChatGPT. None of the cases existed. He faced sanctions.

**Instruction-following hallucination:** The model is asked to summarise a document and adds information not in the document — because it has relevant background knowledge and can't fully distinguish between "what the document says" and "what I know."

**Confidence hallucination:** The model is correct but expresses false certainty — or gives an uncertain answer but presents it as definitive. The calibration is wrong.

**Entity hallucination:** Names, dates, and specific entities get mixed up. "Deepak Chopra, the physicist..." (Deepak Chopra is a wellness author; mixing with a different entity).

## When Hallucinations Are Acceptable vs Dangerous

Not every hallucination matters equally. The key question is: what is the cost of an error?

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              HALLUCINATION RISK BY CONTEXT                           │
├─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────-┤
│                                                                      │
│  LOW RISK (hallucination OK / manageable):                          │
│  - Brainstorming / ideation — wrong ideas just get discarded        │
│  - First drafts — human reviews and corrects                        │
│  - Creative writing — "accuracy" isn't the point                   │
│  - Internal summaries with source doc visible                       │
│                                                                      │
│  MEDIUM RISK (hallucination needs mitigation):                      │
│  - Customer-facing chatbots — wrong info damages trust             │
│  - HR policy Q&A — incorrect policy info has consequences           │
│  - Market research summaries — affects business decisions           │
│                                                                      │
│  HIGH RISK (hallucination is unacceptable):                         │
│  - Medical diagnosis support — wrong info risks patient harm        │
│  - Legal document review — fabricated precedents                    │
│  - Financial advice — wrong numbers cause financial harm            │
│  - Hiring decisions — discriminatory errors                         │
│  - Compliance documentation — regulatory consequences               │
│                                                                      │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Mitigation Strategy 1: RAG (Retrieval-Augmented Generation)

RAG is currently the most widely deployed mitigation for factual hallucinations in production systems.

**How it works:** Instead of asking the model to recall facts from training, you retrieve relevant documents from a knowledge base and include them in the prompt. The model then answers based on the provided documents rather than memory.

```
WITHOUT RAG:
User: "What is ECHO India's overtime policy?"
Model: [generates plausible-sounding but potentially wrong policy text from training data]

WITH RAG:
User question → search knowledge base → retrieve actual policy PDF
→ pass PDF text + question to model
→ model answers based on the provided document
→ model can cite the exact sentence from the document
```

**RAG limitations:**
- Only helps with information that is in the knowledge base
- Retrieval errors (wrong document retrieved) propagate into the answer
- Model can still "hallucinate around" the retrieved content, adding its own knowledge where retrieved content is thin
- Doesn't help with reasoning errors

---

## Mitigation Strategy 2: Grounding and Citations

Force the model to cite its sources explicitly. If every claim must be attributed to a specific passage in a specific document, fabrication becomes detectable.

**Implementation:** Prompt engineering that requires inline citations. Example: "Answer the question using only the provided documents. For every factual claim, include [Doc X, paragraph Y] in the response." Any claim without a citation is a warning sign.

**Limitation:** Models can still generate slightly wrong paraphrases of real citations, or misattribute a quote to the wrong source.

---

## Mitigation Strategy 3: Structured Output and Constrained Generation

For high-stakes outputs, force the model to output in a structured format that makes claims auditable.

Instead of: "Based on my analysis, the candidate has 5 years of experience in Python and has worked at three companies."

Use: A structured JSON output with specific fields pulled from the CV text, with character-level references back to the source document.

**When to use:** Any automated pipeline where claims will be acted on without human review.

---

## Mitigation Strategy 4: Self-Consistency and Verification

**Self-consistency:** Ask the same question multiple times (with varied prompts). If the model gives different answers, it is uncertain — flag for human review.

**LLM-as-judge:** Use a second LLM call to verify the first. "Here is the question and the answer. Does the answer contain any claims not supported by the provided context? List them." Not foolproof (the judge can also hallucinate) but catches many errors.

**Human-in-the-loop:** For high-stakes outputs, always route through human review. This isn't a technical mitigation — it's an acceptance that the technical mitigations are imperfect.

---

## Mitigation Strategy 5: Prompt Engineering Best Practices

Simple but effective:
- "If you don't know, say you don't know." (Models follow this instruction much better than you might expect)
- "Only use information from the provided documents."
- "Do not make up specific numbers, dates, or names. If a specific figure is not in the provided context, state that it is unavailable."
- Temperature control: lower temperature (more deterministic) reduces creative hallucination; does not eliminate factual hallucination

## What This Means for ECHO India

**The ECHO India chatbot risk:** If ECHO India builds an HR policy chatbot that answers from model memory, it will hallucinate policies. An employee acts on a wrong answer about their gratuity entitlement or notice period — that is a real legal and trust problem.

**Mitigation path:** All policy chatbots must use RAG against the current HR policy documents, with citations shown. Every answer should include "Based on [Policy Name, updated YYYY]."

**The CV screening risk:** If an AI summarises a candidate's CV for a hiring manager and the summary contains fabricated qualifications (e.g., inventing a degree or employer not present in the original), a manager acts on it. The mitigation: structured output that maps every claim back to a specific line in the CV.

**The performance review risk:** An AI that "drafts performance review summaries" based on notes may add context not in the notes. All AI-generated performance content should be shown alongside its source material, not independently.

---

## Key Takeaway

Hallucinations are not bugs — they are a structural consequence of how LLMs work. The model generates plausible tokens, not verified facts. Rates range from 2-8% in constrained tasks to 20-60% in open-ended factual recall and citation generation.

The right framework: assess the cost of an error in your context, then choose mitigations (RAG, grounding, structured output, self-consistency, human review) that reduce that risk to acceptable levels. No mitigation makes a system hallucination-free.

For ECHO India, all AI features that surface specific factual claims to employees or hiring managers need RAG + citations as the minimum baseline, with human review for high-stakes outputs (hiring, performance, legal compliance).
