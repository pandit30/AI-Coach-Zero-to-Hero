## Question 1 — Type: Concept Check

Pitching to an AI lab like Anthropic is fundamentally different from pitching to a customer or investor. Describe what you are actually trying to demonstrate — and what the five things are that an AI lab specifically looks for in an enterprise partner.

**What to look for:** You are not selling them on your product. You are demonstrating that your use case: advances their mission, provides meaningful signal about how their models perform in production, and can become a case study that attracts other enterprise customers. The five things AI labs look for: (1) Production scale — 100,000+ end users creates real-world signal; a 50-person pilot does not; (2) Novel or high-impact use cases — not "we use Claude to write marketing copy" but something differentiated like multilingual HR AI for Indian enterprise employees; (3) Responsible deployment — human oversight, bias testing, transparency to users; (4) Case study potential — specific measurable results, willingness to be named, real user quotes; (5) Feedback and collaboration — honest feedback about where the model fails, what features would unlock more value.

Anthropic's mission is "the responsible development and maintenance of advanced AI for the long-term benefit of humanity." This is not marketing — it is the organising principle of the company. What does this mean practically for how you would frame ECHO India's pitch to Anthropic's enterprise team?

**What to look for:** Student should understand that Anthropic's safety focus is genuine (founded because the founders believed AI safety was underweighted at other labs). For ECHO India's pitch: (1) Lead with responsible deployment — human oversight mechanisms, bias testing for HR decisions, transparency to employees that AI is involved in their performance review; (2) Frame the use case in terms of beneficial impact — "better HR outcomes for India's 500M-person workforce" aligns with beneficial AI; (3) Constitutional AI language resonates — "helpful, harmless, and honest" deployment; avoid pitching maximum automation with minimal human involvement; (4) Don't pitch features that Anthropic would be uncomfortable with — emotion recognition, social scoring, high-stakes irreversible automated decisions without oversight. The student should connect Anthropic's identity to specific framing choices.

## Question 3 — Type: Concept Check

What is AWS Bedrock and why is it a strategic entry point for ECHO India's relationship with Anthropic — rather than going directly to Anthropic's API?

**What to look for:** AWS Bedrock is Amazon Web Services' managed AI model platform that includes Anthropic's Claude models as a managed endpoint. Key advantages for ECHO India through Bedrock: (1) Billing on existing AWS account — no new vendor contract for enterprise procurement; (2) Data stays in AWS infrastructure — SOC 2 compliance covered, GDPR/DPDP-friendly deployments, data residency options; (3) AWS is the leading cloud in India — most large Indian enterprises already have AWS commitments, simplifying enterprise procurement; (4) AWS ISV Accelerate program — a pathway to get on Anthropic's and AWS's partner radar, with credits and go-to-market support; (5) Zero data retention agreements available through AWS Bedrock. The strategic logic: many Indian enterprises prefer to use existing vendor relationships (AWS) rather than add a new one (direct Anthropic contract), making Bedrock the path of least resistance for enterprise sales.

## Question 5 — Type: Scenario

Deepak is meeting with Anthropic's enterprise team for the first time. They ask: "Why did you choose Claude over OpenAI for your HR product?" What is the strategically correct answer — and what should Deepak absolutely avoid saying?

**What to look for:** Strategically correct: be honest — the session says "be honest — maybe you tested both." Strong answer: "We evaluated both. Claude's long context window (200K tokens) lets us process complete HR documents without chunking. For enterprise HR, the instruction-following quality and lower hallucination rates matter more than benchmark scores — a wrong answer in a performance review context has real consequences. And Anthropic's enterprise positioning as the safe, capable choice aligns with how our clients think about AI in HR." What to avoid: (1) Vague praise — "Claude is better" without specifics; (2) Disparaging OpenAI — Anthropic's team knows their competitor well; (3) Saying you chose Claude "because of safety" if you haven't actually built safety features — this would be contradicted by the responsible deployment section. The student should demonstrate they know Claude's positioning (long context, instruction following, Constitutional AI, enterprise trust).

## Question 6 — Type: Concept Check

What are the five elements that make a compelling AI lab case study — and which one is hardest for most Indian enterprise companies to provide?

**What to look for:** Five elements: (1) Specific, measurable results — "65% reduction in time-to-shortlist" not "improved HR efficiency"; (2) Real business context — the problem explained clearly enough that similar companies see themselves in it; (3) Before/after design — what was the process before? what is it now? what changed?; (4) Willingness to be named — "ECHO India" is far stronger than "a leading Indian HR tech company"; (5) User quotes from client users — specific individuals with titles and company names. Hardest for Indian enterprise companies: Willingness to be named. Indian enterprises are often conservative about public attribution, especially for AI use cases in HR where there may be regulatory uncertainty or competitive sensitivity. Client relationships and NDA restrictions can prevent this. Student should identify this correctly.

## Question 7 — Type: Application

If Anthropic's enterprise sales team asks about your data privacy requirements, what five things does the session say you should ask for in return — and why does each matter for ECHO India specifically?

**What to look for:** Five questions ECHO India should ask: (1) Enterprise pricing at projected volume — ECHO India needs to model API costs into product unit economics; (2) Data residency options (AWS Bedrock India region?) — ECHO India's clients are Indian enterprises with employee data that may have data localisation requirements under the DPDP Act; (3) SLA and uptime guarantees — an HR SaaS product needs committed uptime from its AI provider; (4) Zero data retention agreements (no training on your data) — critical for ECHO India because client employee data cannot be used to train Anthropic's models; employee data is among the most sensitive categories; (5) Access to Claude's latest models on release day — not 6 months later — competitive positioning requires ECHO India to offer current capabilities; a 6-month lag makes them perpetually behind.

## Question 8 — Type: Scenario

The session identifies four specific differentiators ECHO India has for an Anthropic partnership conversation. What are they — and which do you think is most compelling to Anthropic specifically, given their mission?

**What to look for:** Four differentiators from the session: (1) Scale — India's workforce is 500M; HR tech serving even 1% is 5 million workers; (2) Impact — better performance reviews, fairer hiring, reduced attrition — meaningful improvements in working life; (3) Novelty — multilingual HR AI (Hindi, Tamil, Bengali, Telugu, Marathi) is a use case Anthropic doesn't have many case studies for; (4) Responsibility — enterprise HR is high-stakes; the human oversight design ECHO India builds is a credible safety story. Most compelling to Anthropic given their mission: the student should argue for either Impact (beneficial AI for India's workforce aligns with "long-term benefit of humanity") or Responsibility (Anthropic cares about safety and responsibility genuinely — a partner with real human oversight design for high-stakes HR decisions is exactly the kind of deployment they want to demonstrate).
